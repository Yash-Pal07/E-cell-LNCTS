After oil being the lifeblood of economies and the centrepiece of global power struggle, Lithium has awoken, often dubbed the new oil because of its central role in powering the global shift toward electric vehicles (EVs), renewable energy storage, and digital devices.

Lithium's Strategic Importance

  • Core ingredient for lithium-ion batteries used in EVs, smartphones, and renewable grids.
  • High energy density lithium-ion batteries can store more energy compared to alternatives, making them ideal for EVs.
  • Rechargeability & Efficiency: They charge quickly, last longer, and have lower energy loss.
  • Scalability: From powering smartphones to storing renewable energy on national grids, lithium batteries adapt across industries.
Central to global energy transition and climate commitments like Net Zero targets. Demand is projected to quadruple by 2040, making it a critical resource akin to oil in the 20th century. Whoever controls lithium supply chains will hold immense leverage in the green economy. Even if lithium is mined elsewhere, most of it is processed in China, giving Beijing strategic control and leading to China's dominance in processing and refining. Lithium prices can swing dramatically, influencing EV affordability and renewable energy rollout. Constructing strategic reserves, similar to oil reserves, countries are considering building lithium stockpiles to avoid future shortages.

Global Distribution

  • The Lithium Triangle (Chile, Argentina, Bolivia) holds 60% of known reserves.
  • Australia is the largest producer, with efficient mining and export routes to Asia.
  • China lacks reserves but dominates refining and battery manufacturing.
  • United States and Europe are investing in domestic mines but face environmental and political hurdles.
  • India recently discovered 5.9 million tonnes of reserves in Jammu Kashmir.

Future Trends

  • Battery Recycling & Urban Mining: Advanced recycling facilities, like Northvolt in Norway, are reducing dependence on raw mining.
  • Countries are treating mineral reserves as strategic assets, sparking new alliances and rivalries over lithium control.
  • Latin America may coordinate lithium policies like OPEC did with oil, marking the start of a south-south alliance.
  • Nations and corporations are investing in alternative lithium sources and next-gen battery technologies to make supply chains more efficient, sustainable, and resilient.

From Followers to Communities

NEWS—Earlier, creators measured success in likes, shares, and views. Now, the focus is on community building, moving from anonymous audiences to loyal communities, connecting on Discord, WhatsApp, and newsletters—where engagement runs deeper than a 30-second reel.

Creators as Entrepreneurs

The new-age creator is not just an influencer but a founder in the making. Revenue is no longer limited to ads and sponsorships. Instead, creators are monetizing through subscriptions and memberships, merchandise and D2C brands, paid workshops and courses—entrepreneurship powered by influence. Emma Chamberlain launched her own coffee brand, Chamberlain Coffee.

Edutainment Knowledge Power

Creator Economy 2.0 is also driving the rise of teacher-creators—fitness coaches, coding mentors, finance influencers—who blend knowledge with creativity. Examples include Alakh Pandey (PW), Code with Harry, and Arjun Warikoo.

Passion is the new Startup Capital

Creators as entrepreneurs are more than just a trend—they represent a fundamental shift in the way businesses are built and sustained in the digital era. By turning passion into scalable ventures, they prove that entrepreneurship today is not confined to boardrooms but thrives wherever creativity meets consistency. As the ecosystem matures, creators will drive the future of commerce, influence culture, and fuel innovation.

POWER DIGITAL INFRASTRUCTURE

HOW DIGITAL INFRASTRUCTURE CAN BE USED AS A SOFT POWER?

In today's fast emerging world, the power of a nation is not just about military or economic dominance. The ability to influence others through values, culture, and ideas—known as soft power—has become just as important as traditional forms of strength.

India's Leadership in Digital Infrastructure

One of the most impactful and newest fields of soft power is digital infrastructure. India stands out as a global leader, especially through its Unified Payments Interface (UPI). UPI is low cost, highly scalable, and delivers real-time payments, revolutionizing domestic transactions and now evolving into an international tool for financial diplomacy.

UPI: From Technology to Diplomacy

India’s UPI sets global standards for digital payments:

  • UPI is strengthening ties with partner nations and enabling financial inclusion.
  • The Reserve Bank of India plans to roll out UPI in 20 countries by 2028-29.
  • By mid-2025, 675 to 684 banks had adopted UPI.
  • In November 2024, UPI processed 15 billion transactions per month, accounting for 81.8% of India’s digital payment volume.
  • UPI now powers 50% of all real-time financial transactions globally.
  • UPI integration makes cross-border payments smooth, quick, and feasible, thus driving cultural connectivity.
  • India is standardizing UPI using technology-neutral international norms (FATF) for global applicability.
  • Trinidad and Tobago became the first Caribbean country to officially adopt UPI, showing its expanding global reach.

Conclusion

By exporting UPI, India demonstrates that digital infrastructure is the new soft power. UPI strengthens partnerships, promotes inclusion, enhances cultural ties, and boosts India’s global standing as a digital innovation leader. UPI is now a diplomatic bridge and a “brand ambassador” for India's vision of a digitally inclusive world.

India's Economic Jaipur Odyssey

From Slow Start to Global Powerhouse: India's Journey

India’s economic development started slow and steady post-independence. Between 1947 and 1980, GDP growth lingered around 1-2%, and per capita income barely rose, hovering around 240 INR yearly. The nation was still finding its footing after colonial rule.

The 1980s: Dawn of Economic Revolution

The 1980s marked a turning point with the beginning of economic liberalization. GDP growth surged to 5.5%, and the decade saw the rise of startups like Infosys and Wipro, which laid the foundation for India’s IT industry. Despite challenges such as foreign mishaps and swelling debt, the economy was awakening.

1991-2011: Full Swing Liberalization

The 1990s unleashed free markets, rising incomes, and a flourishing startup culture. India saw a 50% surge in average income, literacy improvements, and GDP growth peaking at 7.5%. Liberalization led to more private investments and integration with the global economy.

2011-Present: Startup Boom and Economic Expansion

Since 2011, India has witnessed a dynamic startup revolution fueling a $4 trillion economy. From InMobi’s breakthrough in 2011 to Byju’s in 2018, India’s unicorn herd has grown to 118 strong, embodying innovation and the “Make in India” vision. The economy is faster, smarter, and more globally competitive than ever.

2025 Outlook: Trillion Dollar Dream Realized

From a modest $30 billion economy in 1947, India is now the world’s 4th largest economy. The journey reflects the power of strategic reforms, liberalization, and human capital development transforming India into a major global growth powerhouse.

Women Entrepreneurs

Breaking Barriers, Building Futures

The rise of rural women founders marks a quiet yet powerful revolution in inclusive innovation. Women entrepreneurs in rural India are no longer waiting for big tech hubs—they are creating opportunities locally, building startups that transform their communities.

Accelerated Growth Amidst Disparity

Women-led startups grow 2.5 times faster than the overall startup ecosystem, signaling a formidable economic force. Despite this, gender and racial disparities persist, with only about 4% of tech roles held by women of color worldwide, emphasizing the urgent need for greater inclusion and support.

Perseverance Pays Off

Female founders face an average of 60 investor meetings to secure funding, demonstrating resilience and determination. Successful stories like Renee Currie, Altai, and Jo Thomas-Enrola inspire new generations of women entrepreneurs and prove their staying power in competitive markets.

The Silent Surge of Rural Women Founders

The number of rural women-led enterprises has steadily increased from 3 million in 2015 to an estimated 11.5 million in 2025, highlighting rapid rural entrepreneurship growth driven by access to technology, microfinance, and community networks.

Diversity: The Ultimate Catalyst for Innovation

  • Ethnically and culturally diverse teams make better decisions and achieve 36% higher profitability.
  • Gender-diverse leadership teams are 35% more likely to financially outperform peers.
  • Broader perspectives drive innovation revenue up by 19% compared to less diverse teams.

Overcoming Barriers

Rural women founders face challenges such as lack of electricity, limited funding, technical training gaps, market isolation, and gender biases. Solutions include solar-powered workstations, microfinance loans, online tutorials through YouTube and WhatsApp, and women-led distribution networks.

Ecosystems Supporting Rural Women

Colleges are launching rural innovation labs and entrepreneurship cells focused on women from underrepresented areas. Peer mentorship networks, skills development programs, and incubators like Atal Innovation Mission and NSRCEL at IIM-B support rural women founders in Tier 2 and 3 cities.

The Moment for Rural Women Founders

This is a perfect time for rural women entrepreneurs as policy reforms, increasing internet access, and focused interventions align. The impact of women founders is spreading, inspiring more to join the entrepreneurial ecosystem.
